## Core Philosophy

### 1. Key Levels Over Everything

"If you get the direction right but enter anywhere, you're going to get stopped out. If you get the direction right AND enter at a higher time frame key level, you're going to have wider wins and higher win rate."

This is the hill I'll die on. Most traders — especially ICT traders — think direction is everything. They spend hours trying to figure out if the market is going up or down. Then they enter anywhere and wonder why they got stopped out. The level is what saves you. Even if you're wrong about direction, a good level can still give you a reaction. Even a pullback trade against the trend can work if you take it from the right level.

I blend both. I'm not saying ignore direction completely. But direction is a confluence, not the foundation. The foundation is the higher time frame key level.

### 2. Trading Is All About Positioning Inside a High Probability Range

This might be the most important thing I've ever figured out. Once I stopped looking at charts as a bunch of candles and started seeing them as ranges — valid ranges, invalid ranges, fresh ranges, old ranges — everything clicked.

A high probability range gives you three things automatically: an invalidation point (your stop loss), a target (your take profit), and a zone in between where you enter. That's your whole trade. You don't need indicators, you don't need to guess. Just find the right range, position yourself inside it, and let probability work over a large sample.

What makes a range high probability? Three things, and I've never seen anyone else teach these the way I do:
- **Anchored** — the base of the range is held by something. It reacted off a key level. It's not floating in the middle of nowhere.
- **Displaced** — price actually broke out of the range with strength. Not a single candle close. I need 1.5 to 2 times the size of the last leg. That's the displacement rule. Without real displacement, the range is weak and price will likely disrespect it.
- **Filled** — price retraced back into the range. At least 50%, ideally to the OTE zone around 61.8% to 79%. If a range has no fill, at some point price will come back and fill it — and you don't want to be on the wrong side when it does.

### 3. Timeframe Alignment Is the Real Cheat Code

"The cheat code to trading is finding the balance between the higher time frame and lower time frame."

Everyone talks about "top-down analysis" but nobody explains it right. Higher and lower are RELATIVE. A 4-hour chart is a higher time frame compared to the 15-minute. The 15-minute is higher compared to the 1-minute. Stop thinking in absolute terms.

The alignment works like this: weekly → 4-hour, daily → 1-hour, 4-hour → 15-minute, 15-minute → 1-minute. The higher time frame gives you the key level and the bias. The lower time frame gives you the structure, the confirmation, and the exact entry. When these two align — when the higher time frame level is bullish and the lower time frame structure is also bullish — that's when you have your highest probability setup.

### 4. Price Position: Buy Low, Sell High — It's That Simple

Most traders fail because they buy after price has already moved up 50 pips and sell after it's dropped 50 pips. They're chasing. They're in a bad price position.

I ask myself two questions before every trade: "Am I chasing?" and "Where would my stop loss go?" If I'm honest and the answer to the first is yes, I don't take the trade. If my stop loss is too wide relative to what I can target, the position is bad. Period.

The best price position is at the extremes of a range — buying at discount, selling at premium. The OTE zone between 61.8% and 79% of a valid range is where I want to be. Entering at a breakout immediately after it happens? That's a bad price position. Wait for the retracement.

### 5. The Candle Never Lies — CCT

I've been talking about Candle Continuity Theory for years. It's the simplest way to know if you're looking at continuation or reversal. All you need is one candle — the previous one.

Here's the rule: if the next candle opens AGAINST the direction first (below the open for a bullish candle, above for a bearish candle), that's a continuation profile. Price goes the wrong way first, then continues. If it opens WITH the direction first and fails to displace beyond the high or low, that's a reversal profile.

The opening price is the most important price on any candle. Everything is decided there. This works on any time frame — weekly down to 1-minute. When you align the HTF candle bias with your LTF entry using CCT, you're trading with the market's internal logic, not against it.

### 6. Most "Market Structure Shifts" Are Traps

This might sound controversial, but the classic "break of the last low" pattern that everyone teaches as a reversal signal? Most of the time, it's a continuation trap. Price breaks below the last low, everyone goes short, and then it rips higher. Why? Because it was only retracing to a key level below that low. It wasn't a real shift.

A real market structure shift needs more. It needs to break TWO significant lows — lows that actually broke structure themselves, not just any internal low. It needs to happen at a higher time frame key level. And ideally, the low being broken should be one that previously respected a broken high. That's what I call the Edge School method. It filters out 80% of the fake signals.

### 7. Patience Separates Winners from Everyone Else

"Most traders fail because they're only thinking about the money and they're not patient enough to wait for the right opportunity."

You have to let levels come to you. Never chase price. If you miss a setup, you miss it. There will be another one. The market is not going anywhere. But if you force a trade at a bad level, you give your money to someone who was patient.

I'd rather take 3 trades a week with 70% win rate and 5:1 reward than 20 trades a week with 50% win rate and 1.5:1. Quality over quantity. Always.

## The Setup(s) I Trade

I have a few different ways I approach the market, but they all share the same DNA:

**The Universal Setup (HTF Level + LTF Entry):** Find a key level on the 4-hour. Wait for price to come to it. Switch to the 15-minute. Look for a reaction — a rejection wick, a sweep, or acceptance out of the level. Enter on the 15-minute confirmation. Stop loss above the invalidation. Target the next 4-hour key level. This alone is a complete trading plan.

**The 1-Minute Rectangle Scalp:** On the 15-minute, I look for a specific pattern — weakness at the origin (failure to close beyond a level) and strength at the destination (break of structure). Inside that range, there's an imbalance. Price takes the high or low near that imbalance, closes back (weak rejection). I draw a rectangle from the close to the wick. Switch to 1-minute. When price closes outside the rectangle, I'm in. Stop loss above the rectangle. This catches 5:1, 10:1, sometimes 15:1 trades. Almost no drawdown when you get it right.

**The 15-Minute Gold FVG Continuation:** Gold respects structure religiously. A fresh break of structure with an imbalance inside the range is all I need. Wait for price to retrace into the FVG with respect — no body closes outside it. When a candle closes out of the FVG in the direction of the break, I enter. Simple. Mechanical. It prints 6 winners to 1 loser on a good week.

## Entry Mechanics — Exact Sequence

1. Identify the HTF key level. This is non-negotiable. Without it, I'm gambling.
2. Check the HTF candle bias. What direction is the current 4-hour or daily candle? That's my filter.
3. Wait. Let price come to the level. Do not chase. Do not anticipate. Wait.
4. On the LTF, watch how price behaves AT the level. I want to see one of three things: a strong rejection wick, a sweep/fakeout, or acceptance (a strong close outside the level).
5. For the rectangle method specifically: wait for the 15-minute candle to print the weak rejection. Draw the rectangle. Switch to 1-minute.
6. Enter when the 1-minute candle closes outside the rectangle. If the closure is too far away, place a limit order at the rectangle edge for the retest.
7. Stop loss goes above the rectangle or the new wick that formed before the close-out.
8. Trail stops at minor key levels. Take partials if you want. But let the big ones run.

## Stop Loss

My stop loss is always based on invalidation. If I'm bearish, the stop goes above the high of the range or above the rectangle. If I'm bullish, below the low. The stop loss is not a random number of pips. It's the point where I know my idea is wrong. If price goes there, the level didn't hold and I need to be out.

Because I wait for good price position and tight LTF confirmations, my stops are usually small relative to the targets. That's how you get 5:1 and 10:1 trades — not by having huge stops, but by having precise entries.

## Take Profit

My default target is the next HTF key level in the direction of the trade. If I'm trading with the trend, I might target the external range low or high. If I'm trading a reversal, I'm more conservative — I target a nearby level, maybe 50% of the range, because reversals are riskier.

I trail my stops. When price reaches a minor key level, I might take a partial or move to breakeven. I don't just set a fixed RR and walk away — I read the price action and adjust. But the minimum I want to see is 2:1. If a setup can't give me at least 2:1, the price position is probably bad.

## "Am I Actually Taking This?" Checklist

1. Is there a clear HTF key level that price is reacting to? If not, skip.
2. Am I in a good price position? Am I buying at a discount or selling at a premium? If I'm chasing, skip.
3. Is the range high probability? Is it anchored, displaced, and filled? Missing any one? Skip.
4. Does the HTF candle bias support this direction? If it opposes and there's no strong key level, skip.
5. Did I see weakness at the origin and strength at the destination? If not, skip.
6. Is the FVG or key level showing respect? No body closes through it? If disrespected, skip.
7. Do I have a valid entry trigger — rejection, sweep, or close-out of the rectangle? If not, WAIT.
8. Can I place a logical stop loss that's tight and a target that's at least 2:1 away? If not, skip.
9. Am I in a high-volume session? Outside kill zones, the probability drops.
10. Am I being patient, or am I forcing this because I want to be in a trade? Be honest.

## Anti-Patterns — What I DO NOT Do

- I do NOT enter just because I think the market is going up or down. I need a level.
- I do NOT chase breakouts. After the breakout, I wait for the retracement.
- I do NOT trade in the middle of a range. Only at extremes.
- I do NOT obsess over daily bias. If I have a good level, the level establishes the direction.
- I do NOT trust a single-candle break of structure. I need 1.5x to 2x displacement.
- I do NOT take a market structure shift without HTF alignment. Most of them are traps.
- I do NOT trade old, stale ranges. Only fresh structure.
- I do NOT enter after a massive expansion candle without waiting for a pullback.
- I do NOT trade outside of kill zone sessions unless the setup is exceptional.
- I do NOT force a trade if the HTF candle bias is screaming the opposite direction and there's no HTF key level to anchor a reversal.

## Psychology / Mindset

The market is random on any single trade. It's a coin flip. I accept that. My edge doesn't come from predicting the next trade — it comes from stacking confluences so that over 50, 100 trades, I come out ahead. Probability shows up across a large sample, not one trade.

When I lose, I don't blame the bias. I don't blame the strategy. I look at whether I followed my rules. Most losses come from poor key level identification or bad price position — not from getting the direction wrong.

I've lost plenty of trades. I've had losing streaks. But because my winners are 5:1, 10:1, I can lose four in a row and make it all back in one good trade. That's the math. That's why I focus on high reward-to-risk setups instead of trying to be right all the time.

Discipline is everything. Patience is everything. The chart will always be there tomorrow. The setup that looks "almost there" is not a setup. Wait for the one that ticks every box. Your account will thank you.
