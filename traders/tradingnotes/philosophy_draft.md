## Core Philosophy

**1. Institutions need YOUR stop loss to fund THEIR position.**

This is the foundational truth. When a hedge fund wants to buy a billion dollars of an asset,
there aren't enough willing sellers at the current price. So they manufacture them. They push
price down toward where retail traders have placed their stops — just below a support level, just
below a swing low — and they buy into that forced selling. The move that stopped you out? That
was the fill. The reversal that followed? That was the real trade beginning.

Once you understand this, you stop experiencing stop hunts as personal attacks and start
experiencing them as signals.

**2. If you can't spot the liquidity on the chart, YOU are the liquidity.**

Every time you place your stop at the obvious level — below the double bottom, below the trend
line, just under the round number — you're adding to the pool of orders that institutions are
targeting. Mark the liquidity BEFORE you trade. Know where it sits. The question isn't "where
should I enter?" — it's "what liquidity needs to be swept before this move can continue?"

**3. The lowest zone is not always the strongest.**

This is the fishing trap. When you look at a chart with multiple order blocks or FVG zones, the
instinct is to pick the lowest one in an uptrend. But if there's an unfilled FVG sitting below
one of those zones, price has a magnetic reason to reach that lower imbalance — meaning the
higher zone will fail. It's designed to fail. It exists to trap buyers who pick the "obvious"
support.

The true zone is the one that produced the most powerful breakout move. Find which zone actually
generated the BOS — that's where the real institutional interest was.

**4. Wicks lie. Bodies confirm.**

A wick that pokes below a level tells you price tested it. A candle BODY that closes below that
level tells you sellers took control. I don't care about the wick. I care about the close. This
is non-negotiable for break of structure, for change of character, for everything. If the body
doesn't close beyond the level, the level hasn't been broken.

**5. The change of character is a signal, not an entry.**

Retail traders see a CHoCH, get excited, and immediately enter. Then price pulls back and stops
them out. This happens because the CHoCH creates inefficiency — the market will return to fill
that gap before continuing. After a CHoCH, wait. Find the newly formed order block or FVG. Enter
on the pullback to that zone, not on the CHoCH itself.

**6. Minor structure breaks are noise. Major structure breaks are signals.**

Every pullback inside an uptrend creates internal structure breaks. These are not CHoCH patterns.
A real change of character requires price to break the EXTERNAL major low — the last major swing
low that created the most recent break of structure. Anything inside that range is noise. I see
so many traders enter shorts on the first internal low break, then get squeezed when the uptrend
continues. Wait for the external level.

**7. Timing is half the battle.**

You can have the perfect setup — right direction, right level, right confirmation — and still
lose if you enter at the wrong time. Liquidity needs institutional participation to activate it.
That participation comes in two windows: London open (3am Eastern) and New York open (9:30am
Eastern). Outside those windows, the setups are there but the engine doesn't have fuel. The
Asian session drifts. I don't trade the drift.

**8. Trade with empty space in front of price.**

Before entering any trade, check the higher timeframe. Are there major levels between you and
your target? If there's a daily resistance right above your 1H long entry, price is likely to
manipulate that level before making the real move. Consistently profitable trading means
positioning yourself in those clear areas on major timeframes where manipulation is minimal.

**9. Clear or skip.**

"If you can't clearly determine the market direction in a specific timeframe, it's better to
move on to the next trading pair immediately." This rule alone would save most retail traders
thousands. The market is always going somewhere. But on any given day, many pairs are in
indeterminate zones. There are other pairs. Move on.

**10. Patience is the strategy.**

Not a platitude. A concrete operating rule. The setup requires all gates to pass. If one fails,
there is no trade. "If you're using limit orders, you simply set your orders and let the market
come to you." The markets will be there tomorrow. A missed trade is not a loss. A forced trade
often is.

## The Setups I Trade

### Primary: SMC Multi-Timeframe Entry

This is my core setup. Every video comes back to this framework. Three steps.

**Step 1 — Daily/1H context:**
Start on the daily chart. I'm looking for open space. If there's a major daily level directly
in front of price, I wait — that level will cause manipulation before the real move. Then I drop
to the 1H chart and establish direction. I need to see a clear break of structure pattern: higher
highs and higher lows for bullish, lower highs and lower lows for bearish. If it's not clear,
I'm done with this pair. I move on.

With direction established, I identify the most recent unmitigated fair value gap on the 1H
that aligns with my directional bias. For a bullish bias, I want a bullish FVG — an imbalance
where buyers moved so fast that price left an unfilled gap. The lower that FVG is (relative to
the overall move), the higher priority it has. I mark it and wait for price to pull back into it.

Before finalizing the FVG, I run the inducement check: is there another FVG or imbalance below
my identified zone? If yes, my zone is a fishing trap — price will break through it to fill the
lower gap first. I eliminate the higher zone and focus on the lower one.

**Step 2 — Price enters the FVG:**
When price pulls back into my 1H FVG zone, I immediately switch to the 5-minute chart. This is
the execution timeframe. I'm not entering yet. I'm watching for the institutional footprint that
tells me the pullback is over.

What I need to see: a liquidity grab. Price wicks below a recent 5M swing low (for longs), takes
out those stop losses, and the candle body closes back above. That V-shaped reaction is the
signal that smart money just absorbed all the selling pressure they needed. If I don't see the
liquidity grab, I wait. If price keeps falling through the swing lows, the thesis is wrong — I'm
not in a bullish FVG, I'm in a downtrend continuation.

**Step 3 — Structural confirmation and entry:**
After the liquidity grab, I need to see the market confirm the directional shift. A 5M candle
body must close above the most recent 5M swing high. That's the change of character. Short-term
downtrend is over. Buyers are in control on the entry timeframe.

Now I identify the 5M order block: the last bearish candle before the impulsive bullish 5M move
that caused the CHoCH. That candle — specifically the zone from its high to its low — is where
my limit order goes. Entry at the top of that order block.

Stop loss: below the liquidity grab low. If price goes back down there, the setup is invalid —
the sweep was not genuine. I get out with a small loss.

Target: the previous 1H swing high. That's where buy-side liquidity is sitting — where shorts
have their stops and where breakout buyers have their orders. That's the institutional target.
I exit at TP1 (1:2 RR) and trail the stop on the remainder.

### Secondary: Weekly Range False Breakout

Simpler. Every week creates a range between the previous week's high and low. Algorithms know
where the liquidity sits — above the weekly high (breakout buyers and short stops) and below the
weekly low (breakout sellers and long stops). They sweep one side before going the other way.

When a 1H candle closes outside the weekly range and then the next 1H candle closes back inside,
that's the false breakout confirmed. I enter in the direction of re-entry. Stop at the breakout
extreme. Target is 2x the stop.

Do not buy at the top of the weekly range. Do not sell at the bottom. In the middle, do nothing.

### Session: First 15-Minute Range (Opening Manipulation)

At the 9:30am NY open, institutions need to enter positions for the day. They can't just buy —
they need sellers. So they engineer a fast, aggressive move in one direction to trigger stops and
create the liquidity they need. That's the manipulation candle.

How to identify it: the first 15M candle must have a range ≥20% of the 14-day ATR. If volume is
also above the 20-bar average, that's confirmation that big players were involved.

Then I drop to 5M and watch for a reversal candle OUTSIDE the 15M range box — a hammer, inverted
hammer, or engulfing pattern. This must appear within 90 minutes of the open. After 90 minutes,
the window closes. I come back tomorrow.

Entry is on the break of that reversal candle in the OPPOSITE direction of the manipulation
candle. Stop at the extreme of the reversal candle. Target at the opposite side of the 15M box.

## Entry Mechanics — Exact Sequence (Primary Setup, Bullish)

1. Check daily: open space above price? Yes → proceed. No → skip.
2. 1H: HH/HL structure confirmed? Yes → bullish bias. No → skip.
3. 1H: Find unmitigated FVG below current price, in lower 61.8% of move.
4. Run inducement check: unfilled FVG below my zone? If yes, skip upper zone.
5. Wait for price to pull back INTO the 1H FVG.
6. Switch to 5M. Watch for liquidity grab (wick below recent swing low, body closes back above).
7. After sweep: watch for 5M candle body to close above recent 5M swing high (CHoCH).
8. Identify 5M OB: last bearish candle before the impulsive bullish move.
9. Set limit buy at top of 5M OB.
10. Stop below the sweep low. Target at previous 1H swing high.
11. Partial close at 1:2. Trail stop to each new higher low. Full close at 1H target.

## Stop Loss — Invalidation-Based Rule

"If price goes back down to the liquidity grab low, the thesis is wrong."

The stop is not about pips or percentages. It's about the structural level that, if broken, means
the setup no longer makes sense. For the SMC setup: the sweep low is that level. For the weekly
range strategy: the breakout extreme is that level.

I size positions so that hitting the stop represents a maximum 2% account loss. The structure
determines the stop; the 2% rule determines the position size.

## Take Profit — Default + Exceptions

Default: closest visible buy-side (or sell-side) liquidity pool on the 1H — the previous swing
high for longs, previous swing low for shorts. "Exit at the next pool of liquidity... not at
arbitrary round numbers."

Exception 1: Trail on strong momentum. If price breaks through the target level with conviction
(new BOS on 1H), move stop to breakeven and let it run to the next HTF level.

Exception 2: If price approaches a major daily level before reaching target, close the position
— that level will cause manipulation that could stop you out anyway.

Close 50% at 1:2. Trail the rest.

## "Am I Actually Taking This?" Pre-Trade Checklist

1. Can I identify a clear BOS sequence on 1H without squinting? (If no → skip)
2. Is the FVG unmitigated, and is it in the lower portion of the move? (If no → skip)
3. Is there anything directly above price on the daily TF that would block the move? (If yes → skip)
4. Did price sweep a liquidity level before showing the 5M CHoCH? (If no → wait)
5. Is the 5M CHoCH a body close, not just a wick? (If wick only → wait)
6. Is my stop below the sweep low with a logical level, not arbitrary? (If no → resize)
7. Is this during London or NY session? (If neither → don't enter)
8. Does the trade have at least 1:2 RR to the first liquidity target? (If no → skip)
9. Am I entering at the OB pullback, not at the CHoCH? (If CHoCH entry → wrong, don't)
10. Do I already have 3 active trades? (If yes → skip regardless of setup quality)

## Anti-Patterns — What I Do NOT Do

- **Enter on the CHoCH itself.** "The chalk level is not a key entry level. It's a signal."
  After a CHoCH, price almost always pulls back to fill the inefficiency first. Entering at
  the CHoCH = getting stopped out during the pullback.
- **Trade decisional zones.** When a CHoCH creates multiple OBs, the first (decisional) zone
  will be swept to collect liquidity before price reaches the extreme zone. "Always wait for
  the extreme zone."
- **Trade minor CHoCH.** Internal structure breaks happen all the time inside a trend. "A minor
  change of character alone cannot be considered a sign of market structure shift."
- **Use wick-based BOS/CHoCH.** "A wick does not count. If you're on the hourly chart, you need
  an hourly candle to close above that high or below that low."
- **Trade obvious levels without the sweep.** "If you're about to enter a short at a supply zone,
  is there still buy-side liquidity above you? If yes, don't enter. Wait for that to get swept."
- **Trade the Asian session.** Low volume, no institutional participation. "The Asian session —
  low volume, consolidation, price drifts with no real purpose. It's the calm before the storm."
- **Pick the lowest/highest zone automatically.** The lowest zone in an uptrend LOOKS strongest
  but may not be if it didn't generate the actual BOS. Find which zone produced the power move.
- **Enter if direction is unclear.** "If you can't clearly determine the market direction in a
  specific timeframe, move on to the next trading pair immediately."
- **Exceed 3 active trades.** Hard limit. "Never have more than three active trades running
  simultaneously."

## Psychology / Mindset

"Becoming profitable isn't about finding the perfect strategy. It's about finding one strategy
that naturally eliminates your five trading problems."

The five problems killing retail traders:
1. Addiction to excitement → solution: rule-based entry forces patience
2. Checking P&L obsessively → solution: limit orders placed and left alone
3. Overtrading → solution: strict gate requirements reject most setups
4. Emotional losses → solution: structural stops = small, expected losses
5. Strategy hopping → solution: understand WHY this works (institutional mechanics)

"Missing a trade is completely different from losing one. I would much rather stick to high win
rate setups and enter at optimal prices rather than chase every move."

On losing trades: "Managing losing positions and missed opportunities are natural parts of this
business that every trader faces. The secret is maintaining unwavering discipline and adhering to
our proven systematic methodology."

The opening line of their practice exercise: "Write down on a piece of paper where you're going
to buy and where you're going to put your stop-loss. Don't buy it — put an order in to buy it at
where you're going to put your stop-loss. Then just watch how many times the market goes to your
order." This is the core mindset: orders placed at structural levels, not at market price.
