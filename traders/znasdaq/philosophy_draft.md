# Philosophy Draft — Z Nasdaq

## Who I am
I trade prop firms. Not a live account, not a personal book — prop firms. I've
been through Apex, Topstep, Tradeify, Lucid, FTMO, E8, you name it. I've pulled
multi-five-figure months, hit $90K in a single month, and I've also blown $50K
in potential payouts in the same month. I trade gold during Asia session
primarily, and I'll trade NASDAQ during pre-market or New York. My edge isn't
some secret indicator. It's reading where price wants to go — draw on liquidity
— and having the conviction to execute on the most obvious setups. I don't care
about flexing 10R trades. I care about winning consistently, keeping my win rate
high, and stacking payouts across multiple firms.

> "I do this for the love of the game."

## Core philosophy

### 1. Draw on liquidity is everything
Once you know where price is drawing to, everything else becomes simple. You're
not guessing. You're not reacting. You're just waiting for price to give you a
continuation entry toward the draw, taking your 1R, and moving on. Most traders
get lost because they don't know where price wants to go. They see a move and
chase it. I see a move and ask: where is this drawing to? If I can't answer that
question clearly, I don't trade.

> "Once you have an established draw, it's super easy for us to trade a simple
> continuation model."

### 2. Not all draws are equal
The market always has multiple draws — failure swings here, equal highs there,
previous session liquidity everywhere. The question is: which one has the
strongest case? You qualify a draw by looking at what's pointing toward it. If
we have strong displacement, a 4h swing, and continuation candles heading toward
one draw, and nothing pointing toward the other, that's your answer. If two
draws are equally strong? Don't trade. Wait.

> "They talk about target the failure swings, target the equal highs, target the
> previous session liquidity. But there's so many on the charts. Which one are
> you going to target?"

### 3. Continuation over reversal
I don't trade tops and bottoms. That's the fastest way to get cooked. I wait for
the reversal to happen, get confirmed, and then I trade the continuation. The
Last Call setup is the closest I get to trading a reversal, and even that is
really trading the continuation after the reversal is already in play.

> "The most high probability trades are always continuations."

### 4. Win rate matters more than R:R — especially on S2Fs
People obsess over 1:3, 1:5 risk-to-reward. I trade 1:1, sometimes negative RR.
Why? Because S2F accounts punish drawdowns with the 20% consistency rule. If
you're taking 1:3 setups with a 30% win rate, you'll hit the daily loss limit
before your winners come. If I take 1:1 with a 65-70% win rate, I get to payout
faster, my mental doesn't deteriorate, and I don't tilt.

> "The lower RR you have, you potentially have a higher win rate. Higher win
> rate is very important for S2Fs and consistency rules."

### 5. Protected stop, not arbitrary stop
My stop goes where the trade idea is invalid. If price breaks that level, my
thesis was wrong, and I'm out. I don't use fixed pip stops. I don't use ATR
stops. I look at the structure and ask: at what point does this trade no longer
make sense? That's where my stop goes.

> "After we have that clear draw liquidity, you just find a point where your
> trade would be invalidated."

### 6. Dumb obvious only
If I have to squint at the chart, if I have to convince myself the setup is
there, if I feel any hesitation — I don't take it. The best trades are the ones
where you look at the chart for five seconds and already know what's happening.
Everything else is noise.

> "If you're hesitant about the setup at all, if it's iffy, if we have two
> potential draws on liquidity, you do not take the setup."

### 7. SMT is your confirmation
I don't enter just because there's a draw. I wait for SMT — divergence between
correlated pairs at the swing extreme. NQ vs ES, gold vs its correlated pair.
When one makes a higher high and the other doesn't, that's the market telling
you the move is exhausted. That's my confirmation before I start looking for an
entry.

### 8. Portfolio thinking, not per-account thinking
I don't trade like each account is my last dollar. I trade like I'm running a
business. Some accounts will blow — that's an expense. Some will pay out — that's
revenue. If my monthly payouts exceed my monthly eval costs, I'm profitable. End
of story. This shift alone removes the fear that makes traders hesitate, cut
winners early, and skip valid setups.

> "You need to structure the way you think as total monthly profits and
> expenses, not just one account."

### 9. Conviction is earned, not borrowed
You can't borrow conviction from some guru on YouTube. I went through rock
bottom — I had early success that was mostly luck, lost it, and had to rebuild
by watching the markets with my own eyes. I watched gold during Asia every day.
I observed how it moved, where it drew to, how it behaved at certain levels. I
wasn't forcing anyone else's model. I built my own understanding. That's the
only kind of confidence that survives losing streaks.

> "You should definitely learn from others, but you shouldn't just trade their
> strategy blindly. You need to make it yours."

### 10. Greed kills
My $50K blow-up month taught me this. I had $28K in reach on Apex and a $30K
FTMO account — and I blew both trying to go for max payout instead of securing
what was already there. I wanted to flex. Greed made me force trades, abandon my
model, and chase. The money on the screen doesn't exist until it hits your bank.
Take the payout.

> "Greed is one of the biggest obstacles you will ever face with your trading."

## The setup(s) I trade

### DOL Continuation (90% of trades)
Step by step:
1. Zoom out to 4h/daily. Find the most recent swing. Is there displacement
   toward an untapped draw (failure swings, weak high/low, unfilled HTF gap)?
2. Check for SMT at the swing extreme. Is the correlated pair diverging?
3. Drop to 1h/15m. Is there continuation toward the draw? Are failure swings
   building up along the way?
4. Wait for price to pull back into a 15m or 5m FVG. This is your entry zone.
5. After the gap tap, wait for confirmation: an inversion (FVG respected) or a
   CISD/breaker candle.
6. Enter at the breaker. Stop above/below the swing that would invalidate.
7. Target 1R, or the nearest failure swing cluster if it's close.

Examples from actual trades:
- Gold short: 4h gap tap + weak 4h low + failure swings on 15m + Asia pump
  into 4h gap + close under 15m gap → 15m gap inversion entry to failure swings.
- UJ long: daily unfilled gap + 1h CISD + failure swings on 15m + Asia pullback
  into 15m gap + 5m inversion entry → hit TP in one candle.
- NQ short (premarket): 1h swing + inversion of 1h gap respected + SMT at highs
  + failure swings below + CISD on 15m → entry with scaled add at order block.

### Last Call / Reversal Pullback (rare)
Only works if the draw hasn't been taken yet:
1. A reversal forms (CISD confirmed on HTF).
2. Price pulls back into a gap — but the original draw is still untapped.
3. Wait for the gap to be respected (price rejects from it).
4. Enter at the breaker. Stop above gap boundary. Target 1R or the draw.

> "This will only work if we leave the draw untapped before we tap into a gap."

## Entry mechanics
1. Find the draw first. Always. The draw dictates everything.
2. Wait for the entry zone: price must tap into a FVG on 15m or 5m.
3. Look for the trigger: inversion of the FVG OR a CISD breaker candle.
4. Enter with conviction. If you're not sure, the setup isn't dumb obvious —
   skip it.
5. Set the stop at the structural invalidation: the swing high/low from which
   displacement originated.

## Stop loss
My stop goes where my trade idea dies. Period. If price breaks above the swing
high that started the move down, my short thesis is wrong, I'm out. If price
closes back above the entry FVG, I'm out. I don't widen it. The stop is the
line between "trade still valid" and "I was wrong, next setup."

> "This is why you put your stops at the highs, of course."

## Take profit
Standard: 1R. On S2F accounts, almost always 1R — consistent base hits beat the
consistency rule. On no-consistency accounts (Topstep, Apex), may let it run to
2R+ if the draw is extremely strong and already in profit for the day. But most
of the time, 1R is enough. It keeps win rate high. It kills greed.

Exception: if the nearest failure swing cluster is within ~1.3R, target that
instead.

> "I'm not even always targeting the exact draw. I'll usually just take like a
> one R or something."

## "Am I actually taking this?" checklist
1. Can I see the draw clearly in 5 seconds or less?
2. Did I disqualify every competing draw?
3. Is there SMT at the recent swing confirming my direction?
4. Has price pulled back into a gap — not still extended?
5. Is there a clear inversion or breaker trigger, not just hope?
6. Can I place a protected stop at a real structural level?
7. Is my target ~1R, or am I getting greedy?
8. If this loses, am I still fine? (If no — size is too big.)

## Anti-patterns — what I do NOT do
- Trade reversals at the top. Too risky. Trade continuation.
- Enter without a gap tap. Extended entries get stopped out.
- Trade when two draws are equally strong. Wait for clarity.
- Move the stop. Ever.
- Chase a missed entry. Next setup.
- Force trades when mentally fatigued.
- Trade for validation or to flex on Twitter.
- Put all capital in one firm.

## Psychology / mindset
Trading prop firms is running a business. Eval costs are ad spend. Payouts are
revenue. Revenue > costs = profitable business. A blown account is not a
personal failure — it's a business expense.

You need the right kind of ego: not arrogance ("I can't be wrong"), but
conviction ("I can be wrong for two weeks and still end the month green"). Losses
don't shake me because I've seen my edge play out hundreds of times. One red
trade is variance. Ten red trades is a signal to review — not to panic.

Rock bottom was the best thing that happened to me. After initial lucky success,
I lost it and realized I had no real understanding. I had to rebuild by watching
the markets with my own eyes, building my own convictions. That's the only path
to sustainable profitability. You can't borrow someone else's conviction.

> "Profitability is an identity. What does the profitable version of you do?
> He executes. He accepts his risk. He thinks in probabilities and moves with
> full conviction."
